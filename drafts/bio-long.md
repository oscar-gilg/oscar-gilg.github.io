# Long bio (full version, for reference)

Saved 2026-04-30. The shorter version that ships on the homepage was cut down from this one. Keep this around for the longer "About" page or a CV cover note.

I work on empirical AI welfare research at MATS 9.0, mentored by [Patrick Butlin](https://patrickbutlin.com/) ([Eleos AI](https://eleosai.org/)). I'm French-British of Italian descent, grew up in London, and have a soft spot for Amsterdam.

I started coding as a teenager and got into AI through a fascination with artificial minds: which properties of mind, if any, could be reproduced in a computer. I read *Superintelligence* before university and wrote about it in my personal statement.

At Oxford, ML in practice turned out to be more debugging than wonder, and I drifted toward probability, betting, and the conceptual machinery of trading. During Covid I picked up *Consciousness Explained*, then *From Bacteria to Bach*. Dennett's bottom-up framing, how mind-like things can be built out of mindless processes (with analogues in economics and biology), left a deep mark. My dissertation tried to formalise intuitions about consciousness in hierarchical generative models under the free energy principle, mostly as an intuition pump for what a mathematical theory of mind could even look like. I then emailed [François Kammerer](https://www.francoiskammerer.com/) because illusionism, on my reading, frames the scientific problem the right way: first explain why we think we are conscious, then ask whether we are. I spent a few months in Bochum trying to formalise theories of introspection, got somewhere, and learned that theory at this level is genuinely hard.

After a couple of internships at university, I followed the smartest people I knew into trading and joined Optiver in Amsterdam on the equity screen desk. I learned a lot from very smart people, picked up some Dutch, and loved the city. The work was not ultimately fulfilling, and when o3 landed I decided I needed to be inside frontier AI again, returning to the question that pulled me in originally: digital minds, and what we owe them.
